\subsection{Founder bio (website-ready)}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{Founder bio (website-ready)}

\textbf{Colm Fallon}

Colm Fallon is a physicist, educator, and founder of Ci\'unas Learning. He holds a PhD in physics and has spent the past decade exploring the intersection of science, education, and human development.

He has spoken at national education conferences, corporate wellbeing events, and schools across Ireland on topics including mindset, resilience, formation, and the implications of artificial intelligence for education. His work draws together scientific clarity and philosophical reflection, with a particular interest in how young people develop judgment, direction, and steadiness in a rapidly changing world.

Colm is the author of \textit{The Climb and the Return} and \textit{AI and the Future of Education}, and previously developed the \textit{Project One Sky} wellbeing programme for schools. His writing and teaching focus on formation rather than performance --- on helping students become thoughtful, responsible, and purposeful in an age of increasing technological abundance.

He lives in Ireland.

\section{Tone and style guide (C + D)}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Tone and style guide (C + D)}

\begin{tcolorbox}[
  colback=white,
  colframe=blue!50!black,
  opacityframe=0.5,
  fonttitle=\bfseries\color{white},
  title={\emoji{brain} Non-Cognitive Skills --- Voice and tone},
  drop shadow
]
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Literary}: measured, human, and reflective, without being vague.
  \item \textbf{Practical}: teacher-facing language that translates into action.
  \item \textbf{Irish and grounded}: avoid Americanised marketing phrasing.
  \item \textbf{Respectful}: assume the reader is busy, intelligent, and mission-driven.
  \item \textbf{Values-forward, not preachy}: integrity, dignity, service, community, and personal responsibility.
\end{itemize}
\end{tcolorbox}

\subsection{Do / Don\textquotesingle t list for web copy}
\addcontentsline{toc}{subsection}{Do / Don\textquotesingle t list for web copy}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Do}: short paragraphs, clear headings, concrete examples, calm confidence.
  \item \textbf{Don\textquotesingle t}: jargon, hype, grand promises, or defensive explanations.
  \item \textbf{Do}: emphasise \textit{teacher-led implementation}.
  \item \textbf{Don\textquotesingle t}: make the programme dependent on the author\textquotesingle s availability.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{AI and the Future of Education}\\
\textit{A 12-lesson workbook for Transition Year}

\medskip

Artificial intelligence can now write essays, solve equations, generate images, and answer questions instantly.

\medskip

\textbf{So what is school for?}

\medskip

This 12-week Transition Year course helps students explore learning, purpose, and responsibility in a world where powerful tools are everywhere.

\medskip

It is not a coding course.\\
It is not a fear-based warning about AI.

\medskip

Instead, it invites students to think clearly about:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Why effort still matters
  \item What cannot be outsourced
  \item How abundance changes expectations
  \item The difference between fluency and understanding
  \item What kind of person they want to become
\end{itemize}

Students develop discussion skills, reflection habits, and ethical awareness.

It fits naturally within Transition Year\textquotesingle s focus on maturity, independence, and personal development.

\medskip

\textbf{Available April 2026}\\
\textbf{School talks available until May 2026 (limited availability).}

\medskip

This version is crisp. It works in:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Brochures
  \item Conference stands
  \item Email follow-ups
  \item Sales conversations
\end{itemize}
\end{tcolorbox}

\textbf{Version 2 --- Publisher website (calm, reflective, philosophical)}

\begin{tcolorbox}[
  colback=white,
  colframe=teal!60!black,
  opacityframe=0.5,
  fonttitle=\bfseries\color{white},
  title={AI landing page copy --- Version 2},
  drop shadow
]
\textbf{AI and the Future of Education}\\
\textit{Learning, purpose, and work in an age of abundance}\\
\textit{A 12-lesson workbook for Transition Year}

\medskip

As artificial intelligence becomes more capable, practical problems become easier to solve. But questions of meaning, responsibility, and direction become more visible.

\medskip

When machines can generate answers, education must ask a deeper question:

\medskip

\textbf{What kind of person should I become in order to use powerful tools well?}

\medskip

This course explores that shift.

Rather than offering technical instruction or dystopian predictions, it creates a structured space for students to reflect on learning, effort, character, and contribution in a world of increasing technological abundance.

Across twelve lessons, students examine:
\begin{itemize}
  \item How comfort reshapes expectations
  \item Why struggle still forms judgment
  \item The difference between performance and formation
  \item The role of non-cognitive skills in an age of automation
  \item Plural paths to contribution and meaning
\end{itemize}

The course is calm rather than alarmist.\\
Philosophical without being abstract.\\
Practical without being vocational.

It aligns strongly with the aims of Transition Year: maturity, independence, and thoughtful engagement with the world.

\medskip

\textbf{Publication: April 2026}
\end{tcolorbox}
