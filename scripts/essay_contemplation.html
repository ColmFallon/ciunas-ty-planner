Contemplation in an Age of Curation

For many years, I was captivated by the idea of enlightenment. I read widely. I meditated consistently. I was searching for a higher state - greater awareness, sharper clarity, some kind of inner breakthrough. Like many in the West, I encountered meditation primarily as a technique: something you could practise, refine, and use to elevate consciousness.

And it worked - in part. It brought calm. It improved focus. It reduced noise. But over time I recognised something I could no longer ignore. Removed from its wider philosophical and ethical framework, meditation had quietly become another instrument of self-optimisation. It was not dissolving the ego; it was refining it. A quieter ego. A more composed ego. A more impressive inner life.

At the same time, I began watching students grow up in a culture that constantly trains them to present a higher version of themselves. Online, identity is curated. Image is managed. Performance is continuous. In that environment, mindfulness can easily be absorbed into the same logic: become calmer, sharper, more centred - a better version of the self you are projecting.

A calmer performance is still a performance.

Ireland has never lacked for depth in its own contemplative tradition. Long before mindfulness became a classroom intervention, men withdrew to remote places along this island's coast not to enhance themselves, but to empty themselves. They did not pursue higher states of consciousness. They pursued surrender.

On islands off our western coast and in early monastic settlements across the country, contemplation was never a standalone technique. It was embedded in community, discipline, work, shared moral language, and a clear sense of belonging to something larger than the individual. Silence was not used to optimise the self. It was used to re-orient it.

This tradition is not a matter of bloodline. It is not genetic, and it is not exclusive. It is cultural. It has always been something that can be entered into. Anyone who lives here, studies here, and participates in the shared life of this country can grow into it. That is how culture endures - not by closing itself off, but by being confident enough to be shared.

What it does require is participation. Culture cannot form a person if it is treated as optional background noise. Contemplative practice cannot form a person if it is detached from a wider framework of meaning. One refines the ego. The other reshapes the person.

Mindfulness in education is still in its early stages. It is often introduced with good intentions - to reduce anxiety, improve focus, and support emotional regulation. These are worthy aims. But during this formative phase, we must be careful not to present it as a neutral technique detached from context.

If mindfulness becomes merely a tool for optimisation - helping students perform better, cope more efficiently, or enhance the identity they present to the world - then we risk reinforcing the very pressures we claim to address.

Educators have an opportunity. We can embed contemplative practices within a shared cultural and ethical framework - one that emphasises humility over self-enhancement, belonging over self-construction, and orientation over elevation.

In an age of curated identities, young people do not simply need techniques to manage the self.

They need formation within something larger than the self.

That responsibility rests with us.
